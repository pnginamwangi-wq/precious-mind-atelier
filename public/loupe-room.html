The Loupe Room
Learn to see like the trade

Eight guided examination pathways, one for each of the great gem materials. Each pathway teaches what the unaided eye can suggest, what a ten-power loupe can reveal, and — most importantly — where honest observation ends and laboratory work begins.

The Loupe Room principle. A loupe raises questions; a laboratory answers them. Nothing you observe here, or at any bench, proves that a stone is natural, untreated, or what its seller claims. Visual inspection narrows possibilities — it never certifies.

Diamond

How to hold a loupe

Bring the loupe to your eye, not the stone to the loupe. Rest the hand holding the loupe against your cheek, bring the stone up in the other hand until it comes into focus about an inch away, and brace both elbows. Stillness, not eyesight, is what separates a clean observation from a guess.

Ten power, by convention

The trade grades clarity at ten-power magnification because it is the internationally agreed reference, not because more power is unavailable. What cannot be seen at 10× does not exist for grading purposes — and what can be seen at 10× still requires interpretation before it means anything.

Inclusions are evidence, not flaws

An inclusion is a record of how and where a crystal grew. Silk in a sapphire, a three-phase inclusion in an emerald, a string of octahedra in a spinel — these are the fingerprints that make a stone individual, and often the very features a laboratory uses to assess origin and treatment.

Know when to stop

The most professional sentence in gemmology is "this needs a laboratory." Synthetics, diffusion treatments, fracture fillings, and lab-grown diamonds can all pass a loupe examination. The loupe's job is to tell you which questions to ask — the laboratory's job is to answer them.
